Key Observations

info 1. Board is Decisively Conservative

The overall mean risk appetite across all scale questions is 1.39 out of 5.0. The Board overwhelmingly favors low risk tolerance across cyber, operational, and AI domains. This is consistent with the Bank's post-MRA regulatory posture.

Assessment implication: The assessment should calibrate severity scoring against a low-appetite baseline. Moderate risks that might be acceptable at a higher-appetite institution become priority findings here.

critical 2. Spending Tolerance Is the Largest Divergence

Q1.6 (cybersecurity underinvestment tolerance) has the widest spread of any question — scores range from 1 to 4. One director believes minimum regulatory spending is acceptable while others insist on investment at or above benchmarks. This is the single most important divergence because it directly affects the Bank's ability to remediate the MRA and fund controls.

Assessment implication: The Board must resolve this disagreement before the Bank can set a cybersecurity budget that reflects Board consensus. This finding should be elevated to the Board Chair for discussion.

critical 3. AI Governance: Prohibition vs. Controlled Adoption

Q5.1 (AI processing confidential data) reveals a structural split: 3 directors say 'No — under no circumstances,' 1 says 'Yes — with Board-approved controls,' and 1 is 'Undecided.' The Bank cannot set AI governance policy when 60% of the Board prohibits AI while the CEO envisions controlled adoption.

Assessment implication: An AI governance framework briefing should be presented to the full Board before the assessment is complete. The assessment should document this divergence and recommend a Board resolution on AI policy.

high 4. Technology Literacy Gap Affects Risk Oversight

One director self-assessed board competency as 'Significant gap — we rely entirely on management' and left 13 questions unanswered, primarily in AI governance and model oversight. This director's responses were consistently 1-2 points more tolerant than the Board mean, and several rationale fields noted 'I do not fully understand' the topic. The director explicitly requested educational briefings.

Assessment implication: The Board should consider: (a) mandatory cyber literacy training for all directors per OCC guidance, (b) recruiting a director with technology or cybersecurity background, and (c) scheduling a Board education session on AI risk before finalizing AI policy.

high 5. Risk Acceptance Authority Is Not Aligned

Q9.4 (risk acceptance authority) shows a structural disagreement: 3 directors support tiered authority (CISO for routine, Risk Committee for material, Board for strategic), 1 director wants all exceptions routed through the Risk Committee, and 1 director believes management should handle all operational risk acceptance with quarterly Board reporting.

Assessment implication: The Board must formally define risk acceptance authority levels. Without this, the Bank cannot demonstrate a functioning risk governance framework to examiners.

high 6. Three Lines of Defense Structure Is Weak

No director selected 'Yes — all three lines are independent and effective.' Responses ranged from 'Partially' (3) to 'No — not clearly delineated' (1) to 'Unsure' (1). Multiple rationale comments flagged: compliance officer reports to CEO (independence gap), internal audit is outsourced with limited IT scope, and IT operations does not formally own first-line risk.

Assessment implication: This is a likely examination finding. The assessment should evaluate the independence and effectiveness of each defense line and recommend structural improvements.

medium 7. Core Banking Downtime Tolerance Varies

Q2.2 (maximum acceptable downtime) ranges from 'Less than 1 hour' (1 director) to '4-8 hours' (1 director). The majority settled on '1-4 hours.' The Bank's current RTO may not align with even the majority position.

Assessment implication: The assessment should verify the Bank's documented RTOs against the Board's stated tolerance and flag any gaps. The most conservative director's position (less than 1 hour for wire transfer systems) has regulatory merit.

info 8. Strong Consensus on PII, Encryption, and Regulatory Risk

The Board achieved near-unanimous consensus (spread ≤ 1 point) on 25 questions. The strongest agreement areas: customer PII protection (mean 1.0), unencrypted data tolerance (mean 1.0), regulatory risk appetite (mean 1.0), and reputational risk (mean 1.0). These represent the Bank's non-negotiable positions.

Assessment implication: Assessment findings in these consensus-low-appetite areas carry maximum weight. Any control gap against a 1.0 appetite score is by definition a priority finding.

Financial Loss Thresholds (Q1.9)

Maximum acceptable financial loss from a single cyber event within 12 months before the Board considers it a threat to safety and soundness:

Director A: $750K. A single event above that would require us to tap capital reserves and would attract regulatory attention.
Director B: $750,000 — approximately 1.5% of Tier 1 capital. This should be formally documented in the risk appetite statement and reviewed annually.
Director C: $500,000 or any amount that would trigger regulatory notification requirements. Whichever is lower.
Director D: $1.5M or approximately 2% of Tier 1 capital. Beyond that the Bank faces safety and soundness questions.
Director E: $1,000,000. That is a round number that would get my attention.
Range: $500,000 to $1,500,000. The Board should formally adopt a single threshold or range for the risk appetite statement. Recommendation: adopt the most conservative director's position ($500K or regulatory notification trigger, whichever is lower) as the Board-approved threshold, consistent with the post-MRA environment.

Assessment Implications

1. Severity calibration. With overall Board appetite at 1.39/5.0, the assessment will use a low-appetite baseline for severity scoring. Control gaps that might be rated "moderate" at a higher-appetite institution will be rated "high" here.

2. Priority areas. Any control gap in a consensus-low-appetite area (PII protection, encryption, regulatory readiness) is automatically a priority finding. The Board has spoken with one voice on these topics.

3. Board action items before assessment completion:

  • Resolve the cybersecurity spending tolerance divergence (Q1.6)
  • Establish a Board position on AI governance (Q5.1) — prohibition vs. controlled adoption
  • Define and document risk acceptance authority levels (Q9.4)
  • Formally adopt a financial loss materiality threshold (Q1.9)
  • Assess Board cyber competency and consider training or recruitment (Q10.5)
  • Evaluate three lines of defense independence (Q10.6)

4. Disclaimer. This summary is an analytical input to the assessment. Management validation is required before Board-stated appetite positions are used to support specific findings. The assessment team will verify alignment between questionnaire responses and the Bank's documented risk appetite statement and Board resolutions.
